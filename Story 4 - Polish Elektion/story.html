Polish parliament system

Poland has a bicameral parliament consisting of two houses: The Sejm: The lower house, which is more powerful and plays a major role in lawmaking. It consists of 460 deputies (members) who are elected for a four-year term. The Senate: The upper house, with 100 senators also elected for a four-year term. The Senate's role is primarily to review and amend the bills passed by the Sejm, although it has less power compared to the Sejm. Let`s focus on the Sejm . For the Sejm, Poland uses a proportional representation system. Voters choose from party lists in multi-member constituencies, and seats are allocated according to the percentage of votes each party receives. The political landscape is diverse, with several parties typically represented in the Sejm. The two major parties in recent years have been the Law and Justice Party (PiS) and the Civic Platform (PO), now part of the larger Civic Coalition (KO).

PiS Backstory

Funded in 2001 by two twin brothers Lech and Jaroslaw Kaczynski, The party was created on a wave of popularity gained by Lech Kaczyński while heading the Polish Ministry of Justice, Both brothers played a prominent role in Polish politics, with Lech Kaczyński later becoming President of Poland in 2005 until his tragic death in a plane crash in 2010.

PiS's political ideology is often characterized as conservative, nationalist, and Eurosceptic. Its platform combines a commitment to traditional, Catholic social values with a strong emphasis on social welfare, state intervention in the economy, and law and order. The party is also known for its scepticism towards further European integration and its stance on preserving Polish sovereignty within the EU framework.

In its early years, PiS was part of the broader coalition government. The party's popularity grew steadily, appealing to voters who were disillusioned with the liberal economic policies of previous governments and who favoured a more traditional, patriotic narrative. PiS first came to significant power in 2005, when Lech Kaczyński was elected President and the party formed a coalition government after the parliamentary elections. This period marked the beginning of its influence in shaping Poland's domestic and foreign policies. Since its founding, PiS has played a major role in Polish politics, often polarizing public opinion. Its policies and approach to governance, particularly regarding judicial reforms and media freedom, have been subjects of both domestic and international scrutiny and debate.

Civic Platform Backstory

Civic Platform was founded in 2001 as a center-right political party. It was established by Andrzej Olechowski, Donald Tusk, and Maciej Płażyński. The formation was a response to the perceived need for a moderate, pro-European Union political force in Poland, positioned as a counterbalance to the more conservative Law and Justice Party (PiS) and the post-communist Democratic Left Alliance (SLD).

Originally, PO positioned itself as a liberal-conservative party with a strong emphasis on free-market economics, European integration, and modernization of the state. The party appealed to middle-class voters, entrepreneurs, and those supportive of Poland's integration into the European Union.

PO quickly became one of the leading political forces in Poland. Its popularity surged in the mid-2000s, particularly under the leadership of Donald Tusk. Tusk's tenure as the party leader and his premiership (2007-2014) marked the peak of PO's influence in Polish politics.

PO formed a government after the 2007 parliamentary elections, leading to Donald Tusk becoming Prime Minister. During its time in power, PO focused on economic liberalization, administrative reform, and strengthening Poland's position within the EU. Tusk's government was also noted for its pragmatic approach to foreign policy, especially relations with Germany and Russia. In the face of rising competition from PiS and changing political dynamics, PO sought to broaden its appeal. This led to the formation of the Civic Coalition in 2018, an electoral alliance spearheaded by PO but including other smaller parties and political movements. The aim was to create a more united front against the increasingly dominant PiS. Despite its successes, PO (and later KO) faced various challenges, including internal divisions, controversies over certain policies, and a perception among some voters that it had become disconnected from the needs of ordinary Poles. This was partly attributed to its strong pro-EU stance and liberal economic policies, which contrasted with the more nationalist and welfare-oriented approach of PiS.

PiS controversies

Judiciary under government control

Julia Przylebska, a former judge and diplomat, was one of the most well-known recipients of such an appointment when she has been appointed president of Poland's constitutional court in 2015. A few standard procedural steps were omitted, making the appointment controversial.

Attack on the media independence

Following PiS's electoral victory in 2015, the government took control of Poland's state media, which since then has continuously promoted a favourable image of the ruling party and highlighted matters that the party considers significant. Journalists who refused to follow this new direction were forced to leave or face dismissal.

Targeting migrants and the LGBTQ+ community

PiS has specifically agitated against two groups in an effort to influence public opinion in its favour: immigrants and members of the LGBTQ+ community. President Andrzej Duda, a close associate of PiS, stated in 2020 that the LGBTQ+ movement is an ideology rather than people.

Restrictive abortion laws

The strengthening of Poland's abortion regulations, which were already among the most restrictive in Europe, is another significant legacy of the PiS administration. Poland's constitutional court decided in favour of a law that only permits abortion in cases of rape and a threat to the mother's life or health in October 2020, despite widespread protests. The new rule requires parents of children who are terminally ill or severely disabled to bring their newborns to term.
